About

Hannah is a fifth-year graduate student at the University of Missouri (anticipated grad. May 2020). She is interested in the health consequences of being a member of a minority group and particularly how repeated experiences of discrimination act as a stressor. Her dissertation investigates the temporal dynamics of the relationship between racial discrimination and mood, affect, and mental health using ecological momentary assessment (participants report their mood, affect, and experiences with discrimination repeatedly over the course of 28 days). She’s also interested in individual differences like coping styles and vigilance that may play a role in the relationship between experiencing discrimination and health outcomes and uses EEG to measure neurocognitive responses to threat.

Additionally, Hannah is interested in early perceptual processes related to social categorization. Research suggests that faces are judged as male, female, gay, straight, White, Black, Asian, etc. within hundreds of milliseconds. Regardless of their accuracy, these category judgments have profound implications for downstream evaluations, expectations, and behavior. One might think that such category decisions are solely derived from bottom-up information perceived in the face, such as skin color, eye shape, or hair texture. However, top-down influences have been shown to influence categorization decisions, especially for people whose group memberships are ambiguous. Hannah is interested in how elements of the perceiver (learning history, expectations, associations with contextual clues) influence how the perceiver categorizes the person they’re seeing, which then affects what how they evaluate and interact with them.

Hannah believes in a multimethod approach and uses neuroimaging (EEG and event-related potentials), speeded-response tasks in the lab, and self-report to understand how race and gender distinctions affect and are affected by perception, motivation, previous learning experiences, and attitudes.
